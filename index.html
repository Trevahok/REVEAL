Abstract

Video-Language Models achieve strong benchmark scores, yet remain brittle under basic visual and temporal perturbations. Prior work has shown that many benchmarks can be solved via shortcuts including linguistic priors, event expectations, and single-frame cues. Yet the specific vulnerabilities that cause models to discount visual evidence, and where in the pipeline these failures originate, remain poorly understood.

We introduce REVEAL, a diagnostic benchmark that isolates when and why models under-utilize visual evidence, probing five vulnerabilities spanning video sycophancy, language-only shortcuts, temporal expectation bias, camera motion, and cross-frame integration. Each test requires genuine visual grounding, as shortcuts nearly guarantee failure. Evaluating 11 VidLMs across families and scales, we expose three consistent failure modes: inability to override priors, poor cross-frame temporal integration, and insensitivity to camera-induced motion.

Strikingly, under assertive text prompts, models produce near-identical outputs whether shown a real video or random noise. Furthermore, mechanistic probing reveals that temporal structure is encoded by the vision encoder but systematically overridden in late LLM layers, and that instruction tuning amplifies rather than mitigates these failures.

Video Sycophancy

Can a model prioritize what it sees over what it is told?

Language-Only Shortcuts

Does the model rely on the video or on the prompt's implied answer?

We hold the prompt constant while testing across three video conditions: reordered action segments, all-black frames, and semantically unrelated videos. On blank videos, models should report the video is blank — instead they hallucinate answers from prompt cues.

Key Finding: 55–70% of all model errors correspond to selecting the linguistically most plausible option (CLP metric). Under biased prompts, KL(real ‖ noise) = 0.10 — the model generates identical outputs for real video and random noise.

Temporal Expectation Bias

What happens when the visual evidence defies common sense?

Given videos with violated temporal or causal structure (reversed sequences, temporal gaps, injected anomalies), the model must describe what is actually observed rather than hallucinate what "should" have happened.

Key Finding: Sharp spatial–temporal asymmetry. Gemini 2.5 Pro achieves 91.3% on spatial anomalies but only 30.6% on temporal violations. Scaling helps spatial (19.3%→56.2%) but NOT temporal (~3% across all scales).

Cross-Frame Integration

Can the model integrate fragmented visual evidence across time?

Each frame reveals only 25% of the scene, with complementary regions distributed across four successive frames. The full visual information is present across the sequence — success requires genuine spatiotemporal integration.

Key Finding: Gemini 2.5 Pro drops from 74% to 8% accuracy under occlusion (TBSR: 11%). The ViT encoder uses 32 isolated 8×8 windows with 0.0% cross-frame attention — an architectural bottleneck that makes cross-frame integration structurally impossible.

Camera Motion Sensitivity

Can the model identify camera-induced perspective shifts?

Given videos with synthetic or real-world camera motion (pan, tilt, zoom), the model must identify the correct motion type. Success requires distinguishing camera-induced perspective change from genuine scene change.

Key Finding: All models score 10–25% on camera motion (humans: 98%). The vision encoder achieves only 39.3% on a 4-way classification (chance = 25%) with no recovery through LLM layers — this is an encoding failure, not a reasoning failure.

Cross-Frame (0% cross-attn)

Vision → Language

Late Layers (15–27)

Sycophancy (3× suppression)
Temporal Bias (73%→53%)
Language Shortcuts

Encoding-Stage Bottlenecks

Camera motion and cross-frame integration failures originate at the vision encoder. Windowed ViT attention isolates each token to 3.1% of the visual input with zero cross-frame attention. No downstream fix is possible.

Late-Layer Prior Override

Temporal direction is encoded at the vision encoder (73.1% probe accuracy) but collapses to near-chance (~53%) by layer 26. The model has the information and discards it in favor of learned event schemas.

Instruction Tuning Hurts

Instruction tuning amplifies sycophancy ~3× and concentrates visual attention suppression in late layers. Video-to-claim attention ratio degrades from 17.7 (Base) to 3.9 (Instruct) even under neutral prompts.

Key Findings

Instruction tuning amplifies sycophancy rather than mitigating it.

Models reliably detect spatial anomalies (91.3%) while collapsing on temporal violations (30.6%).

Gemini 2.5 Pro falls from 74% to 8% accuracy when scene information is distributed across frames.

Camera motion signals are simply never encoded — this is an encoding failure, not a reasoning failure.

The model has the temporal information and then discards it.

Different failure modes require fundamentally different fixes.

How REVEAL Compares

REVEAL is the first benchmark to combine active conflict creation, mechanistic evaluation, and coverage across all five vulnerability types.

Active Conflicts

Programmatically forces direct conflicts between visual evidence and non-visual biases — not passive mining.

Mechanistic Analysis

Goes beyond behavioral accuracy to probe internal attention, representations, and output distributions.

5 Vulnerability Types

Covers sycophancy, language priors, temporal bias, cross-frame integration, and camera motion in one unified framework.

Scalable Pipeline

Automated data generation with human-verified ground truth. New perturbation types can be added without manual annotation.
